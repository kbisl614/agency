## 1. Big‑Picture CRM Frustrations (Across Tools)

### 1.1 General sentiment about field‑service CRMs

- “I’ve tried HubSpot, Jobber, ServiceTitan, and Housecall Pro… **they all feel like they were designed by people who have never run a field service business.**” [web:47]  
- “Every one of these CRMs wants to be your ‘all‑in‑one operating system,’ but **I’m still duct‑taping calendars, phones, and spreadsheets together.**” [web:47]  
- “The more we ‘automate,’ **the more time I spend babysitting the software instead of running calls.**” [web:47]  

**Themes:**

- Over‑complex “all‑in‑one” platforms that still leave gaps. [web:47]  
- Office‑centric workflows that don’t match techs in the field. [web:49][web:50]  
- Reliability problems with data, phones, and reporting. [web:49][web:53]  
- Sales‑driven vendor behavior (aggressive selling, hard to cancel). [web:35][web:50][web:53][web:56]

## 2. Jobber – Detailed Pain Points

### 2.1 Complexity and workflow friction

- “Jobber sucks I’m extremely disappointed with Jobber. **It is overly complicated**, with too many loopholes for invoices. I’m stuck reopening jobs…” [web:35]  
- “Overall it gets the job done, **but with a number of frustrations** that I’m hoping they work through the kinks.” [web:38]  

**Implications:**

- Extra steps and “loopholes” around invoicing create rework. [web:35]  
- Users tolerate Jobber because it “gets the job done,” but with friction. [web:38]  
- There is a notable learning curve before it feels “intuitive.” [web:40]  

### 2.2 Bugs and data accuracy issues

- “Time tracking is faulty and does not add time together properly… it often gives around **90 min extra time per week** to my team… I’ve had to quit using it.” [web:38]  
- “We have several customers who’ve explained the same frustration, especially when it comes to **using their own data (or not being able to access it)**… if anyone’s used Jobber, I’m sure you’ll understand.” [web:49]  

**Implications:**

- Owners don’t fully trust Jobber’s time and job data. [web:38][web:49]  
- Risk of billing errors, payroll errors, and mis‑calculated job‑costing. [web:38][web:49]  

### 2.3 Messaging and automation limitations

- “The text feature is great, **but you cannot access it from the computer**, and in order to send a message you have to have previously sent that client a message and **the only allowable messages to start a text is that you are late or going to be late.**” [web:38]  

**Implications:**

- Communication tools are rigid and template‑bound. [web:38]  
- Hard to use Jobber as a flexible automation layer for lead capture, quote chasing, cancellations, etc. [web:38]  

### 2.4 Support, cancellation, and vendor relationship

- A user describes having to **email multiple times and call during US business hours** just to cancel Jobber, hitting an “out‑of‑hours message” due to time zones, and only “eventually” cancelling. [web:35]  

**Implications:**

- Cancellation is perceived as high‑friction and inconvenient. [web:35]  
- Fear of being stuck in an unwanted subscription. [web:35]

## 3. Housecall Pro – Detailed Pain Points

### 3.1 Data migration, onboarding, and trust

- “Turn & RUN. Loved our Onboarding specialist & **that's the only good thing I have to say about it.** Sales lied & said **our client data would be able to be transferred** to them. It wasn't – after waiting over 30 days for it to ‘transfer’ & then finding that the majority of it was NOT transferrable & **having client addresses all mixed up.**” [web:50]  
- “Then they wouldn't refund us the 2 months of payments we'd made, because they have a 30 day guarantee… **we couldn’t USE it.** She was like, oh well – that's our policy… **This program seems like a scam to me.**” [web:50]  

**Implications:**

- Expectations around data import and “go‑live” timelines are often broken. [web:50]  
- Data corruption (mixed‑up addresses) destroys trust immediately. [web:50]  
- Owners feel burned paying for months when the product is unusable. [web:50]  

### 3.2 Bugs, reliability issues, and VoIP failures

From Trustpilot:

- “They are constantly adding new features and changing things instead of **working out the many known bugs and issues that slow down**…” [web:53]  
- “Features didn’t work as expected, and **customer support was slow to respond** and not very helpful when they did… Overall, it feels **unreliable and not worth the cost.**” [web:53]  
- “I`m paying for a **VoIP service with them** and been using it for 2 years but I have **no service for the past 30 days**, for a business it`s a disaster and **we already lost more than 20k dollars on the customers we lost.** The support found a bug and confirmed that it`s on their side but **they don't know how to fix it.** We're switching to another service and moving all our database which is a hustle.” [web:53]  

**Implications:**

- Owners resent “new features” when core bugs remain unresolved. [web:53]

### 3.3 Aggressive sales, upsells, and feeling harassed

**Implications:**

- Owners equate persistent cold‑calling and refusal to stop with “scam‑like” behavior. [web:50][web:53][web:56]  

### 3.4 Office vs technician experience

- r/HVAC comment: “Good for office **horrible for techs**… sometimes makes me want to throw my damn phone.” [web:50]  

**Implications:**

- Office staff may like features; techs in the field often hate the UX. [web:50]  
- Any added friction for techs (slow app, extra taps) generates resentment. [web:50]

## 4. ServiceTitan – Detailed Pain Points

### 4.1 Complexity, setup, and implementation hell

From r/HVAC “What’s your biggest problem with Service Titan?”:

- “It’s not set up in any fashion to be streamlined into a company. Between the **convoluted design** and the **piss poor assistance** from the company or its 3rd party contractors… We are nearing 3 months being live and **we still don’t have an updated price book that was supposed to be there when we went live.** I wouldn’t recommend it to someone who doesn’t have **the patience of a saint. Stick to paper.**” [web:49]  
- “Same problem with our company. **3 months in and still figuring out how to get data over from our old system.**” [web:49]  
- “It never ended up being complete. **The business shut down before ServiceTitan got its shit together** partially due to the lack of assistance from the ServiceTitan reps and the PE group that bought the company and fucked everything up.” [web:49]  

**Implications:**

- Implementation can drag on for months, disrupting operations. [web:49]  
- Price‑book and data migration are consistent choke points. [web:49]  
- At least one owner ties business failure partly to a ServiceTitan rollout. [web:49]  

### 4.2 Performance and field‑use frustration

- “Buffering time as you add in information, **inability to close/dispatch in bad service areas**, when it wipes my page clear and refreshes.” [web:49]  
- “My problem was the system **making us fill out useless forms**. My favorite was when you clicked ‘arrive’ on the job site an ‘arrival’ checklist would appear and one of the mandatory questions was **‘did you click “arrive” when you arrived at the job?’** Took years for the place to remove it.” [web:49]  

**Implications:**

- Online‑only, heavy interfaces break down in poor cell coverage. [web:49]  
- Mandatory, redundant checklists create strong user resentment. [web:49]  

### 4.3 Permissions, bugs, and unintended side‑effects

- “Giving access to ‘Settings’ gives users access to **update Company information, job types, forms, etc. It's awful.** We have submitted several bugs with them from things they changed during an update that weren't mentioned anywhere in their release notes.” [web:49]  
- “If a technician is offboarded… and a user needs to update a custom field in a job, the **deactivated technician is removed from the Lead tracking.**” [web:49]  

**Implications:**

- Permissions are coarse; it’s easy for staff to change critical configuration. [web:49]  
- Updates can introduce bugs and changes without clear communication. [web:49]  
- Hidden logic (e.g., offboarding techs affecting lead tracking) erodes trust in reporting. [web:49]

## 6. Automation & AI‑Related Gaps and Expectations

### 6.1 What “automation” means in practice for these tools

- Jobber: automation mainly around **appointment reminders, quote follow‑ups, invoice nudges, and review requests**, while the office still has to “manage, monitor, and **adjust the schedule from a single screen**.” [web:37][web:40]  
- Housecall Pro: after‑hours call handling uses **AI‑generated voice technology and templates**, but calls “follow a template aligned with your terms of service,” not a fully adaptive dispatcher. [web:20]  
- ServiceTitan (via Scheduling Pro / Dispatch Pro): can auto‑assign many calls (~75%) but is part of a larger, complex ecosystem that has already caused multi‑month implementation pain for some shops. [web:57][web:49]  

### 6.2 Deeper expectations and anxieties around AI layers

From the above, owners worry that:

- “Automation” still requires lots of manual supervision (“babysitting the software”). [web:47]  
- Vendor‑controlled core systems (VoIP, payments) can fail spectacularly (e.g., 30 days of no service, “more than 20k dollars” lost). [web:53]  
- New features and AI rollouts break existing workflows or introduce bugs. [web:49][web:53]  

At the same time, they clearly want:

- Faster lead response and fewer missed calls. [web:21][web:24]  
- Less dispatcher chaos and fewer manual schedule edits. [web:6][web:40]  
- Automation that removes clicks and headaches instead of adding them. [web:49]

These phrases and stories are the raw VOC you can mirror when positioning an AI/automation layer that:

- Sits **on top of** Jobber / ServiceTitan / Housecall Pro (no migration).  
- Doesn’t touch the risky infrastructure (like core VoIP and accounting).  
- Removes dispatcher work and lead leakage without adding forms, checklists, or multi‑month projects.
